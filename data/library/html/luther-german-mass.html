Preface

In the first place, I would kindly and earnestly beg all those who see this order of service or desire to follow it: Do not make it a rigid law to bind or entangle anyone's conscience, but use it in Christian liberty as long, when, where, and how you find it to be practical and useful. For we are not publishing this for the sake of those who are already Christians; they need none of these things for their salvation. We publish it for the benefit of the simple and the young, who must be trained and educated in Scripture and the Word of God daily, so that they may become familiar with it, able in it, well versed and skilled in it, ready to defend the faith and to answer every one that asketh them a reason of the hope that is in them, as Saint Peter teacheth 1 Pet. 3:15.

For such, one must read, sing, preach, write, and compose; and when it will help and promote it, I would assemble all bells, and all organs, and everything that can make sound, into service. For the service of the Antichrist has so grossly abused these matters that I detest them, or I would not, for all the gold and silver in the world, advise doing away with them for the purpose of helping Christians.

In the second place, we had considered three different kinds of divine service or Mass:

The first is the Latin service, which we published some time ago under the title Formula Missae. I do not wish to have this one abolished or changed. As we have hitherto employed it, so would we still keep it where it pleases us or where we must, since it is not in every way displeasing to me. For the young are to be trained in it, too.

The second is the German Mass and order of service, of which we are now treating. It should be arranged for the sake of the plain people.

The third kind is that which we ought seriously to have among ourselves in private — a truly evangelical order in which those who seriously desire to be Christians and confess the Gospel with their lives should sign their names and assemble by themselves in a house to pray, to read, to baptize, to receive the sacrament, and to do other Christian works. In such an order, those who are not living in Christian fashion could be recognized, admonished, corrected, expelled, or excommunicated. Here one could also impose on Christians a general obligation to give charitable offerings, which would be distributed to the poor according to St. Paul's example in 1 Cor. 16:1–2. But I am not yet able or prepared to order such a congregation; for I have not the right people for it yet, and I do not see many who want it.

The order is to be arranged as follows:

The Sunday Service

For the Sunday service among the people we arrange matters as follows:

First, the service begins with a good hymn or Psalm. In German we have now enough good hymns. All the parts of the Mass are to be sung in German. The Introit may be a psalm or a hymn.

Then the Kyrie eleison, threefold, as printed in the book, not tenfold as heretofore. From this we may proceed immediately to the collect, or the Gloria in excelsis may be added. But for the time being, I think it best to let the Kyrie stand alone until we have more German songs.

Then a collect in a clear voice, spoken in this form: "O Lord, our Father, the God and Saviour of our souls, who by Thy Holy Spirit hast awakened and gathered us into Thy congregation to hear Thy Word: Grant and bestow on us, we pray Thee, that we may hear it and keep it with sincere hearts, and that we may grow day by day in the grace and knowledge of Thy Son Jesus Christ, until we come to the full measure of His perfection. Amen." Or some other collect, according to the season.

Then the Epistle is read. The Epistles and Gospels currently used in the Mass I have not yet had the power to change. I am not satisfied with them, for men have selected short pericopes from the Apostles; they ought properly to have taken whole Epistles and the complete Gospels. In place of the Epistle the German congregation might also sing a hymn.

Then a German hymn is sung, either "Now Let Us Pray to the Holy Ghost" or some other, in place of the Gradual. This may be done well.

Then the Gospel is read in a clear voice and distinctly. After the Gospel the whole congregation sings the Creed in German: "We all believe in one God" — in verse form. This is very necessary; for in this way all the people come to know the faith and to sing it together.

Then comes the sermon on the Gospel for the Sunday or festival day. I think that, if a sermon in German on the Gospel is given every Sunday and on feast days, this is enough for the ordinary Christian who must work for his living. Besides this, time may be given on certain days for the reading of an Epistle or a chapter from the Old or New Testament.

After the sermon the whole congregation sings together: "Lord have mercy upon us" — the German Sanctus or the song "Isaiah, in a vision did of old." Then shall be announced publicly what the Lord's Supper will be held, so that those who wish to receive it may prepare themselves.

Then the Lord's Prayer is prayed publicly in this paraphrase: "O Lord God, our Father, who art in heaven, look graciously down upon us poor sinners, and make us with hearty confidence call Thee our Father and to boast that we are Thy children, in order that Thou mayest be moved to hear our prayer. Give us today our daily bread, and let us not depend on or trust in our own care, toil or work — for we commit all this to Thy Providence; but guard us from covetousness and the cares of the belly. Forgive us our trespasses, as we also forgive those who trespass against us — for we will not hold grudges, want vengeance, or be bitter; but forgive gladly and serve our neighbor for Jesus' sake, who is our Advocate. Deliver us from evil, for the devil and his kingdom have no power over us. Thine is the kingdom, the power, and the glory, forever and ever. Amen."

Then the Words of Institution of the Lord's Supper are sung or read in a clear voice, that the people may hear them:

"Our Lord Jesus Christ, in the night in which He was betrayed, took bread; and when He had given thanks, He brake it and gave it to His disciples, saying: 'Take, eat; this is My body, which is given for you; this do in remembrance of Me.' In the same way He took the cup after supper, and when He had given thanks, He gave it to them, saying: 'Drink ye all of it; this cup is the new testament in My blood, which is shed for you for the forgiveness of sins; this do, as often as ye drink it, in remembrance of Me.'" 1 Cor. 11:23–25

The distribution takes place while German hymns are sung — "Jesus Christ, Our Blessed Savior" or "O Lord, We Praise Thee" — so that the people thereby have something to do and to sing during the communion. The pastor distributes the bread and the cup.

After the distribution, the congregation gives thanks, a collect is read, and the congregation is dismissed with the Aaronic blessing or the one from Numbers: "The Lord bless you and keep you; the Lord make His face shine on you and be gracious to you; the Lord turn His face toward you and give you peace." Num. 6:24–26

Weekday and Evening Services

For the weekday services — particularly those held for the sake of the young in the schools — we arrange matters as follows:

In the morning, at Matins, they sing several Psalms in German, along with a Latin Responsory or Antiphon with a collect, as we have always done. The Psalms are well suited for the morning. After the Psalms, a lesson is read from the Old Testament — a chapter or two. Then a German hymn. Then the Lord's Prayer and a collect, and they are dismissed with the customary blessing.

For Vespers or the evening service, a psalm is sung in German; then a lesson from the New Testament, a chapter or two. After this, the Magnificat in German, or a German hymn. Then the Lord's Prayer in brief, and a collect with the benediction.

We can sing Compline also in German, with the hymn "Keep Us, Lord, by Thy Dear Word," or "The Day Is Now Past, Lord" — whichever one knows and is accustomed to.

As for Baptism and Marriage, forms of service in German have already been published. For Baptism I have written two orders — a German Baptismal booklet and a revised form. For Marriage, a German order also exists. Let each use whichever form he thinks best.

On Vestments and Ceremonies

As for vestments, I have not yet come to a fixed conclusion. Ceremonies, however, are not matters of great importance, and we shall not make laws about them. Let each congregation do as it finds best, and where there is liberty, let no one coerce another. Only let all things be done decently and in order 1 Cor. 14:40, and let the service be given heartily and in sincerity.

The upshot of the whole matter is this: Everything should be done so that the Word of God may have free course and not be bound, as Saint Paul says in 2 Tim. 2:9. The external order of worship is altogether free, and may be changed as circumstances demand; but the preaching of the Word and the administration of the Sacraments are to be kept pure and uncorrupted.

This is the German Mass and the order of divine service which we have now drafted and published. Let each use it as best he can, where and when he can, with good conscience. In short, I admonish all, and especially the ministers and preachers, to be diligent in their office, to study Scripture, to exercise themselves in preaching and instruction; and particularly among the young — for the benefit of those who come after us — to spare no trouble, so that, with God's help, a truly Christian congregation may be gathered and sustained.
