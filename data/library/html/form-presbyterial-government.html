Introduction

The Form of Presbyterial Church Government was adopted by the Westminster Assembly of Divines in 1645 and approved by the General Assembly of the Church of Scotland. Together with the Westminster Confession of Faith and the Westminster Directory for Public Worship, it defines the polity of Presbyterian and Reformed churches. It establishes the offices of the church, the nature of congregational and broader assemblies, and the principles of church discipline.

Of the Parts of Ecclesiastical Power.

There are two parts of that power which the Lord Jesus, as King and Head of his church, hath appointed to be exercised by church-officers.

The first is authoritative, which is either more general, which is proper to all church-officers, as the power of order, or more particular, which is proper to some officers, as jurisdiction.

The second is ministerial or declarative. It is a ministerial and declarative power, reaching to all the members of the church: namely, to open and shut the kingdom of heaven, to bind and loose, to receive and exclude, by virtue of the word and ordinances of God, with prayer.

Of the Officers of the Church

Christ hath instituted a government, and governors ecclesiastical in the church: to that purpose, the apostles did immediately receive the keys from the hand of Jesus Christ, and did use and exercise them in all the churches of the world upon all occasions.

The officers appointed by Christ in this his church are some extraordinary, as apostles, evangelists, and prophets, which are ceased. Others ordinary and perpetual, as pastor, teacher, and other church-governors, and deacons.

Of Pastors. The pastor is an ordinary and perpetual officer in the church, prophesying of the time of the New Testament. It belongs to his office, to pray for and with his flock, as the mouth of the people unto God, Acts 6:2–4; to read the Scriptures publicly; for the easier understanding of them, to expound any chapter ordinarily fallen in his reading; or text chosen by him for that purpose; to feed the flock, by preaching of the word, according to his gift and their necessity; to apply the word by wise counsel to particular persons according to their various necessities; to administer the sacraments; to bless the people from God, Num. 6:23–26; 2 Cor. 13:14; to take care of the poor; and to rule well in common with other elders, 1 Tim. 5:17.

Of Teachers or Doctors. The Scripture doth acknowledge teachers, as well as pastors, to be ordinary church-officers. It is the duty of the doctor of the church, to open up the Scriptures; to expound out of them sound doctrine, with the confutation of errors; and, for the more easy understanding of the holy Scriptures, to be skilled in the original tongues, and in such arts and sciences as are thereunto subservient.

Of Ruling Elders. As there were in the Jewish church elders of the people joined with the priests and Levites in the government of the church; so Christ, who hath instituted government, and governors ecclesiastical in the church, hath furnished some in his church, beside the ministers of the word, with gifts for government, and with commission to execute the same when called thereunto, who are to join with the minister in the government of the church, Rom. 12:7–8; 1 Cor. 12:28; 1 Tim. 5:17.

Of Deacons. The Scripture doth hold out deacons as distinct officers in the church, Phil. 1:1; 1 Tim. 3:8; whose office is perpetual. To whose office it belongs not to preach the word, or administer the sacraments, but to take special care in distributing to the necessities of the poor of the church, with simplicity, Acts 6:1–4; Rom. 12:8.

Of the Congregational Assembly

Ordinary assemblies are, first, congregational, which is a meeting of the officers of a particular congregation, with any other fit persons, to consult and determine in the affairs of that congregation.

The congregation, with their officers, may regularly meet by themselves for the exercise of discipline, the administration of the word and sacraments, the election of their officers, and other affairs peculiar to their own congregation.

It belongs to the congregational assembly to judge of such members of the congregation, in matter of discipline, as are not officers; and of officers, in matter of discipline, if they be not ordained to the higher assembly. It belongs also to the congregational assembly to elect and ordain their own officers; but their election is to be with the consent and approbation of the Presbytery.

Particular churches are member-churches of classical presbytery, and do stand in relation thereto.

Of the Classical Assembly (Presbytery)

The next ordinary assembly is classical, which is a meeting of the pastors and ruling elders of a certain number of congregations near adjoining, for the exercise of jurisdiction over all the congregations within their bounds.

The classical assembly consists of all the pastors, and one ruling elder out of each congregation within the bounds of that assembly.

To the classical assembly it belongs,

  1. To ordain pastors, teachers, and ruling elders, within their own bounds, after due examination and trial of gifts.
  2. To examine and approve, or censure, the proceedings of the congregational assemblies within their bounds.
  3. To receive and determine appeals and references from the congregational assemblies.
  4. To appoint solemn times of humiliation and thanksgiving within their bounds.
  5. To ordain fasts in cases of great necessity within their bounds.
  6. To exercise ecclesiastical jurisdiction over all congregations and persons within their bounds.
  7. To send commissioners to the provincial and national assembly.

Of Provincial and National Assemblies

The next ordinary assembly is provincial, which is a meeting of the classical assemblies within a certain province, by their commissioners.

A provincial assembly consisteth of an equal number of ministers and ruling elders, delegated by the several classical assemblies within that province.

To the provincial assembly it belongs,

  1. To receive and determine appeals and references from classical assemblies.
  2. To ordain solemn days of humiliation and thanksgiving, and fasts, within their bounds.
  3. To examine and approve, or censure, the proceedings of classical assemblies.
  4. To take care that the directory for worship be observed, and to judge in matters of doctrine and worship brought before them.
  5. To send commissioners to the national assembly.

The supreme ordinary assembly in the church is the national assembly. A national assembly consisteth of commissioners, both ministers and ruling elders, delegated by the several provincial assemblies.

To the national assembly it belongs,

  1. To receive and determine appeals and references from provincial assemblies.
  2. To judge of controversies of faith and cases of conscience.
  3. To give authoritative and judicial sentence in matters of doctrine.
  4. To take care of the government of the church throughout the nation, and to judge of the proceedings of provincial assemblies.

Of Ordination and Church Discipline

Of Ordination. Ordination is the solemn setting apart of a person to some public church office. It is to be performed with prayer and laying on of hands by those who are set apart to the government and ministry of the church.

Ordination of pastors is to be performed by a presbytery, which is to try them as to their fitness for the ministry; to pray over them; and to lay hands upon them, ordaining them to the office of the holy ministry in the name of the Lord Jesus.

Of Church Censures. Church censures are necessary, for the reclaiming and gaining of offending brethren; for deterring of others from the like offences; for purging out of that leaven which might infect the whole lump; for vindicating the honour of Christ, and the holy profession of the gospel; and for preventing the wrath of God, which might justly fall upon the church, if they should suffer his covenant, and the seals thereof, to be profaned by notorious and obstinate offenders.

The censures of the church are admonition, suspension from the sacrament of the Lord's Supper, excommunication, and, where it is agreed upon by the church, deposition from office.

Excommunication is the most solemn and severe censure of the church. It is to be used only in case of such offences as, after the due use of other censures, remain still obstinate. It is the exclusion of the offender from the sacraments and from the fellowship of the church.

Restoration to the fellowship of the church is to be pronounced by the minister, in the face of the congregation, upon credible evidence of the repentance of the excommunicated person.
