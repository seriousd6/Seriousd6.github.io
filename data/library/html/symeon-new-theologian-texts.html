Discourses — On the Conscious Experience of Grace

Symeon the New Theologian (949–1022) was a Byzantine monk and abbot at St. Mamas in Constantinople, acclaimed "New Theologian" — the third to receive this title after St. John the Evangelist and Gregory of Nazianzus — because his theology arose entirely from personal mystical experience. His Catechetical Discourses (Κατηχήσεις) were delivered to his monks, insisting that the living experience of the Holy Spirit is not an extraordinary privilege but the ordinary birthright of every Christian who repents and seeks God with a pure heart.

Discourse I — On Repentance and the Awareness of God

Brothers, I beg you, let us not deceive ourselves. The whole purpose of Christian life is to receive the Holy Spirit visibly and consciously, to be illumined by Him, and thus to become sons of God and heirs of the Kingdom of Heaven. This is not a matter for the next life only — it begins here.

I am not speaking about extraordinary visions reserved for the few. I am speaking about what is given to every Christian who truly repents and seeks God. Our Lord said: "The one who loves me will be loved by my Father, and I will love him and manifest myself to him" (John 14:21). Do you hear? He will manifest Himself. This is not a promise made to prophets and apostles only; it is made to all who love Him.

Why then do we not experience this? Because we do not repent fully. We confess our sins with our lips and return to them with our lives. We receive the sacraments but do not purify our hearts. We read Scripture but do not hunger for the God it reveals. Brothers, God does not hold back His grace from us. We hold it back from ourselves.

True repentance means more than remorse for sins. It means a complete turning of the whole person — mind, will, and desire — away from created things and toward God. It means weeping for sin not merely because we fear punishment but because we grieve over our separation from the One who loves us. It means watching the movements of the heart and refusing to consent to any thought that draws us away from God. This is the work of a lifetime. But those who persevere in it will not be disappointed. They will know God — not by hearsay, not by reading, but by experience.

Discourse II — On the Light of the Holy Spirit

The Holy Spirit is Light. This is not a metaphor; it is a reality. When the Holy Spirit comes to dwell in a person who has been purified, He illumines the person from within — not with a bodily light, but with a divine light that the intellect perceives in a manner beyond all sensory experience. The saints have described this again and again, and they have not been wrong.

The Prophet David, filled with this light, cried out: "In your light we see light" (Ps. 36:9). The Apostle Paul, struck by it on the Damascus road, fell to the earth (Acts 9:3–4). He spoke of knowing a man "caught up to the third heaven" — and the Fathers understand this as the illumined intellect entering the divine presence. Our Lord Himself was transfigured on Tabor in this very light, and the disciples fell on their faces before it (Matt. 17:2–6). This light is the energy of God, His uncreated grace, which He shares with those who are made pure.

Some will say: these things belong to the apostolic age. I say to you: God does not change. The Holy Spirit does not change. The divine light does not change. What has changed is the intensity of our seeking and the depth of our purification. If the saints of every age have received this gift — and they have — then we too may receive it, if we will pay the price of love, prayer, and watchfulness.

Discourse III — On the Spiritual Father and the Path of Prayer

I teach you nothing new. I only remind you of what the whole Church has always known: that prayer is the breath of the soul, and without prayer the soul dies. But I speak especially of that prayer which is prayer of the heart — when the mind descends into the heart and prays there with the whole person, not merely with the lips.

The Jesus Prayer — "Lord Jesus Christ, Son of God, have mercy on me, a sinner" — is the prayer of the heart. It unites the name of Jesus with every breath, every heartbeat. As the Apostle commands: "Pray without ceasing" (1 Thess. 5:17). How is this possible? Only when prayer has descended from the lips into the heart, so that the heart itself is always praying, even when the lips are silent and the hands are at work.

To learn this prayer, you need a guide — a spiritual father who has himself walked this path. Do not trust yourself without guidance. The spiritual life is full of deceits, and the soul that relies on itself is easily led astray. Find a guide. Submit to him. Confess to him not only your deeds but your thoughts. And pray without ceasing.

Hymns of Divine Love

Selected Hymns of Divine Love

Symeon's Hymns (ΎΜνοι) are poems of mystical experience, among the most personal and direct theological writing in the Christian tradition.

Hymn I — How shall I contain Thee?

How shall I contain Thee, who art unencompassable?
Where shall I take Thee, who art everywhere present?
How shall I think of Thee, who art unthinkable?
How shall I approach Thee, who art unapproachable?
How shall I see Thee, who art invisible and intangible?
How shall I speak of Thee, for who can speak of God?

And yet — Thou comest to me. Thou who art beyond all approach
dost approach me. Thou who art beyond all sight
dost make Thyself seen. Thou who art beyond all union
dost unite Thyself with me, who am unworthy of Thee.
And all this Thou dost not because I have earned it
but because Thou art love itself, and love seeks its own.

Hymn XV — On the Indwelling of the Holy Trinity

Come, true light.
Come, eternal life.
Come, hidden mystery.
Come, treasure without name.
Come, reality beyond all words.
Come, person beyond all understanding.
Come, rejoicing without end.
Come, light that knows no evening.
Come, unfailing expectation of the saved.
Come, raising of the fallen.
Come, resurrection of the dead.
Come, all-powerful, for Thou dost always make and re-make and transform by Thy will alone.
Come, invisible, wholly intangible, and utterly beyond all touch.
Come, Thou who dost remain forever unmoved, yet every hour dost move Thyself toward us
who lie in Hades, Thou who art in the highest heavens.

Come, Thou who art desired by my poor soul.
Come, Thou who art the solitary to the solitary, for I am alone, as Thou seest.
Come, Thou who hast become my desire, and hast made me desire Thee who art beyond all reach.
Come, my breath and my life.
Come, the consolation of my humble soul.
Come, my joy, my glory, my endless delight. Amen.

Hymn XLIV — On the Mystery of Deification

I know that I am unworthy — more unworthy than all men.
Yet I know that He loves me, for He has given Himself to me,
and this gift which He has given is beyond my comprehension.
He has given Himself entirely — not a part of Himself,
but all of Himself entirely. God has given Himself to man.
And man has received God. This is the mystery.

He who is fire has united Himself to me, who am tinder;
and I, a strange wonder, was not consumed but instead illumined,
and I was made, O wonder of wonders, to share in His nature,
fire mingling with fire, light with light, God with god —
and I remain what I am, yet am transformed into what He is.
Two become one, one remains two. This is the mystery of love.
