What Are the Psalms?

The Psalms are Israel's official prayer book — used in temple worship, synagogue liturgy, and private devotion from antiquity to today. Unlike the rest of the Bible, in which God speaks to humanity, the Psalms are primarily humanity speaking to God. They are inspired speech addressed to God, not about him — though they reveal much about him in the process.

They cover anguish and adoration, confession and confidence, betrayal and vindication, national catastrophe and personal joy. They teach us how to pray by showing us prayer in practice: prayer that does not domesticate God or airbrush the sufferer's reality.

The Psalter's Architecture Five Books mirroring the Torah Rabbinical tradition linked the five-book structure to the five books of Moses. The Psalter opens with Psalm 1 (wisdom instruction about the Torah) and closes with five "Hallelujah" psalms (146–150). The journey from individual lament to corporate praise is the Psalter's arc.

Major Psalm Types

Form criticism identifies several recurring psalm types, each with its own characteristic structure and purpose. Recognizing the type helps a reader hear what the psalm is doing — not just what it says.

Lament (individual & corporate)
The largest category (~65 psalms). Structure: address, complaint, petition, expression of trust, vow of praise. Often moves from "My God, why?" to "I will praise." Examples: Pss 22, 42, 88.
Praise / Hymn
Corporate worship of God for who he is or what he has done. Structure: call to praise, reasons for praise, renewed call. Examples: Pss 8, 19, 100, 145–150.
Thanksgiving
Individual or corporate response to a specific deliverance — the flip side of lament. "I was in trouble; I cried out; God answered; now I testify." Examples: Pss 30, 116, 138.
Royal Psalms
About the Davidic king — coronation, battle, wedding. After the exile they become messianic: pointing to the coming King. Examples: Pss 2, 45, 72, 110.
Wisdom Psalms
Reflective, instructional — often comparing the righteous and wicked, or meditating on Torah. Examples: Pss 1, 37, 73, 119.
Penitential Psalms
Confession of sin and cry for mercy. The church has traditionally numbered seven: 6, 32, 38, 51, 102, 130, 143. Ps 51 is the locus classicus.

The Gift of Lament

The Psalms' most countercultural contribution to contemporary spirituality may be the lament. The dominant lament pattern — "How long, O LORD?" — is not a failure of faith but its exercise. The psalmist cries out to God precisely because he believes God is present, capable, and obligated by covenant relationship to act. To demand that God explain himself is to treat him as real.

The church has largely lost the language of lament, replacing it with either forced celebration or silent suffering. The Psalms give voice to both the cry and the confidence, often in the same poem — and insist that both belong in prayer.

My God, my God, why have you forsaken me? Why are you so far from saving me, so far from my cries of anguish? My God, I cry out by day, but you do not answer, by night, but I find no rest. Psalm 22:1–2

The Psalms and Jesus

The New Testament quotes the Psalms more than any other Old Testament book. Jesus' dying words are Psalms 22:1; his entry into Jerusalem fulfils Psalms 118:25–26; Psalms 110:1 is the most quoted OT verse in the NT. The early church read the Psalms as a prophetic script that Christ embodied and fulfilled.

This means the Psalms can be read at multiple levels: as the prayers of David, as the prayers of Israel, and as the prayers of (and about) the greater Son of David. Reading Psalm 22 on Good Friday is not a misreading — it is the fulfillment the psalm was pointing toward, even while expressing genuine historical suffering.

Discussion Questions

For individual reflection or group study. The Psalms are meant to be prayed, not just studied — consider reading the featured psalms aloud before answering.

Book-Level Questions

  1. The Psalms contain prayers of raw anguish, anger, and doubt — yet they are considered inspired Scripture. What does this say about what God welcomes in prayer? What kinds of honest speech do you hold back from God?
  2. Many psalms move from lament to praise within the same poem (e.g., Ps 22). What prompts this movement? Is it a change in circumstances, a change in perspective, an act of will, or something else?
  3. The Psalter was Israel's liturgical book — most of these prayers were composed for corporate worship, not just private devotion. How does reading them as corporate prayers change their meaning? What is lost and gained in praying them alone vs. together?
  4. The Imprecatory Psalms (e.g., Psalms 137:8–9) call down judgment on enemies. These are uncomfortable. How do you handle them? What does it mean that they are in Scripture? Can you pray them honestly?
  5. Psalm 1 and Psalm 150 frame the entire Psalter: the first meditates on Torah; the last calls everything that breathes to praise. What does this beginning and ending say about what the whole Psalter is doing? What journey does it invite?
  6. Jesus quotes the Psalms more than any other Scripture. What does it mean to read the Psalms as prayers that Christ himself prayed? How does that change Psalm 22 ("My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?") or Psalm 23?
  7. Which psalm have you returned to most often in your life? What season of life drew you to it? What does that psalm reveal about what you needed from God at that time?

Key Psalm Questions

With 150 chapters, the Psalms are best studied by type and key example. These questions focus on representative psalms from each major category.

Psalm 1 Wisdom

The "blessed man" meditates on Torah day and night, like a tree planted by streams (vv. 2–3). What is the connection between constant meditation on Scripture and the fruit that follows? What does that meditation look like practically?

Psalm 2 Royal / Messianic

The nations rage against God and his anointed (vv. 1–2). God's response is laughter — then installation of the King on Zion. How does God's sovereign calm in this psalm speak to situations where his purposes seem threatened?

Psalm 22 Lament / Messianic

Psalm 22 opens with "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" and closes with "He has done it" (v. 31). Jesus cried both from the cross. What does the journey from abandonment to vindication tell us about the shape of faith?

Psalm 23 Trust

"Even though I walk through the valley of the shadow of death, I will fear no evil, for you are with me" (v. 4). The shepherd's presence is the reason for fearlessness — not the absence of danger. What "valley" are you walking through? Does the shepherd's presence change how you experience it?

Psalm 51 Penitential

"Against you, you only, have I sinned" (v. 4). David says this after sinning against Uriah and Bathsheba. What does it mean to say sin is ultimately against God, when real people were harmed? How does this reframe what repentance requires?

"Create in me a clean heart, O God, and renew a right spirit within me" (v. 10). Why does David ask for creation (bārāʾ — the word used for God's original creating)? What does this say about what genuine repentance requires?

Psalm 73 Wisdom / Doubt

Asaph nearly stumbles when he sees the prosperity of the wicked (vv. 2–3). What restores his perspective is entering the sanctuary (v. 17). How does worship reorient what you see? What have you found that shifts your perception when the wicked seem to prosper?

Psalm 88 Dark Night

Psalm 88 is the only psalm that ends without resolution — "darkness is my closest friend" (v. 18). Why is this psalm in the Bible? What does it offer to someone for whom the usual movement from lament to praise does not come?

Psalm 119 Torah Love

The psalmist says "Oh how I love your law! It is my meditation all the day" (v. 97). He does not treat Torah as a burden but as a delight, a treasure, and a source of freedom. What would it mean for you to experience Scripture that way? What stands between you and that experience?

Psalm 130 Penitential

"With you there is forgiveness, that you may be feared" (v. 4). Forgiveness produces reverence — not presumption. What does this say about how knowing you are forgiven is supposed to affect you?

Psalms 146–150 Closing Doxology

The Psalter ends with five pure praise psalms, each beginning and ending "Praise the LORD!" The final verse: "Let everything that has breath praise the LORD!" (Ps 150:6). After 149 psalms of struggle, why does the book end here? What does it suggest about where a life of prayer is meant to arrive?
